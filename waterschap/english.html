Introduction

This is a guide to the election on 15 March 2023, written for people who moved to the Netherlands in 2022.

Whether or not you plan to stay in the Netherlands, you have the right to shape the country’s future by voting in this election. But this may be the first time you have even heard of a water board, and it’s even possible this is the first chance in your life to vote.

This booklet will try to explain what water boards are, what they do, and why they are worth voting for. It will tell you how the electoral system works and give practical instructions on how to cast your actual ballot. It will invite you to consider a few topics of debate, and gives the location of an interactive questionnaire which can help you decide how to vote.

Much of the information contained in this booklet is adapted from material published originally in Dutch by the Union of Water Boards and the Dutch national government. It is published in Ukrainian, Russian and English by Benjamin Goodger of Amersfoort.

Water boards

Water boards (Dutch: waterschappen) are the part of the state that is responsible for management of water in the Netherlands. This is a big job in this country in particular. The Netherlands is formed mostly of a single enormous river delta with a quarter of a million kilometres of waterways. After a millennium of land reclamation and drainage projects, over 50% of the country is less than a metre above sea level. The Dutch say, “God created the world, then we created the Netherlands.”

Starting in the 1200s, democratic institutions were entrusted to keep the sea and rivers in check. Now the entire country is divided into 21 water boards, and they maintain dams and dikes that stretch for a total of 18,000 kilometres. They are also responsible for sewage treatment, but they don’t supply drinking water to our homes.

Governance and funding

The structure of a water board mirrors that of a Dutch municipality (gemeente). Each water board has a general council, an executive team chosen from that council, and a chairperson (equivalent to a mayor) who is appointed by the national government. The chairperson has the residual title of “dijkgraaf”, meaning “earl of dikes.”

The composition of the general council tries to balance the needs of the general population, farmers, businesses and Nature. In the case of the Vallei en Veluwe water board, which contains Amersfoort, the 30-member council has 23 members elected by the residents of the area. The other seven seats are “reserved” for interest groups of farmers, businesses, and nature organisations.

In contrast to municipalities, water boards do not routinely take grants from the national government. Instead they directly tax each household and business in their area. The amount of tax paid depends partly on the amount of sewage produced.

Politics of water

Every water board has problems unique to the territory that it controls. But they all have to deal with problems that affect the Netherlands as a whole, or all of Europe, or the whole world.

Europe now experiences extreme weather on an annual basis. You probably remember last summer’s drought, but the summer of 2021 brought deadly flooding to several countries. The damage to the Netherlands was over €1.2 billion with no fatalities, whereas just over the border in Germany, the same flood killed 196 people.

Such tragedies of course show the exceptional competence of Dutch emergency management, but they also show how vital it is to ensure that flood defences are properly designed and maintained. In other words, people need to be sure that the water boards are doing their job properly because corruption, negligence and apathy are all deadly.

There has not been a devastating flood in the Netherlands for seventy years, in part because of massive investment in flood defences in the last century. But the sea level is rising and patterns of rainfall are becoming less predictable. At the same time, there is an urgent need for new housing and an increasingly difficult conversation about water pollution.

Do we need higher, stronger, costlier dikes? Should land be set aside for the water? Can we build houses wherever we like? What’s more important, agricultural jobs or clean water? Should we invest now, or wait to see where investment is most badly needed? And should we spend money on improving access to water-based recreation? These are all questions that water boards have to answer.

Why vote?

You don’t have to vote. But you should, if you want to.

Rules of the election

The Dutch electoral system has several important properties designed to make it free and fair, meaning that elections produce results that accurately reflect the genuine wishes of every eligible voter.

Every voter gets one vote. Trying to vote more than once in the same election is a serious crime. The stempas is a single-use document that proves to election workers that you haven’t already voted at a different polling station.

Voting is no longer compulsory.

Eligibility

There are actually two elections scheduled in the European part of the Netherlands on 15 March 2023. (And there are two more elections in the Caribbean Netherlands on the same date). The other election happening here is for the provincial councils, and voters must have Dutch nationality to vote in it.

Voting for the water board is open to all adults living legally in the Netherlands. If you are a refugee of the Russo-Ukrainian war, you count as a legal resident. So, to vote, your date of birth must be 15 March 2005 or earlier and you must be registered in the BRP database.

You can only vote for your own water board – that’s the one which contains your registered home address (in the BRP database) on 30 January.

Some people are banned from voting in the Netherlands. But this can only happen to someone who is convicted of specific serious crimes. You would know if it had happened to you (and you would be in prison).

Electoral mathematics

Dutch elections use what’s known as multiple-winner proportional representation with open party lists – the same system used to elect half the members of the Ukrainian parliament in 2019.

Multiple-winner means that the vote is expected to produce more than one winning candidate. In the case of the Vallei en Veluwe water board election, this number is 23. Each of the 23 winners needs the same proportion of overall votes (about 4.4% in our example).

Each vote is for a candidate, not for a party. But a lot of “extra” votes are left over when a candidate gets more than 4.4%, or not enough to win. Those votes will effectively go to the party. The list ordering determines which candidates get the extra votes.

This arrangement means that the proportion of winners from each party will very closely reflect the proportion of votes for the parties’ candidates, even if the individual candidates receive very different numbers of votes.

If you want to vote for a party and don’t particularly care about the candidates (the majority opinion), then you can choose to simply vote for the first candidate from your chosen party. Some people prefer to vote for the first woman or the first man on the list. Or you could vote for a specific candidate that you like, even if that candidate has little chance of winning. In neither case is your vote wasted.

Independent candidates are permitted and are treated like the only candidate in a party of one.

Privacy

Your vote is private. Election workers, police officers, party officials, your parents, siblings, spouse, children, employer, landlord – all remain ignorant of the way you voted. You are perfectly free to tell anyone how you voted, but they have no way to check. It’s the only way to ensure you feel safe voting the way you really want.

Photography and video recording are not permitted inside the polling station for this reason.

The only record is that you received a ballot. The ballot itself is completely anonymous, designed to show exactly one vote and nothing else. It must not be signed, annotated or otherwise marked in any way, or it will be rejected during the count.

Before the election

Besides deciding how to vote (see below), preparing for the election is very simple for almost everyone.

The invitation

If you are eligible to vote, you should receive an A5 postcard labelled “STEMPAS” before the end of February. This is your invitation to vote. It has your name and address, basic information about the election, and a number of security features. On the back is a form you can fill in if you need someone else to vote in your place (see below).

Don’t throw the stempas away! You will need it to vote. If it is lost or damaged, you can get a new one by visiting your city hall before noon on 14 March (the day before the election).

The candidate list

Shortly after the invitation, you should receive a list of the candidates in your area. The list is very similar to the real ballot: a large folded paper with candidates (possibly hundreds of them) arranged in columns, one column per party. The party’s name is at the top. Then each candidate is listed, like this:

The candidate’s family name is printed in bold, opposite their position in their party’s list of candidates. Below that are the candidate’s initials and their name (possibly a nickname, so the initial doesn’t necessarily match). An m or v then indicates that the candidate is male or female, respectively. Finally, the candidate’s city of residence is printed at the bottom right.

There are no party logos, photographs, messages to the voter, or descriptions of the candidates. It is considered impossible to ensure the objectivity of such material, so it cannot be included in official documents.

Polling stations

It’s a good idea to choose a polling station in advance. Almost certainly you can find one within walking distance of your home. Your stempas will give the addresses of several of them, but (depending on where you live) you may have more options. You can choose the most convenient – for example, one that is between your home and workplace.

Most polling stations will open at 7:30 and close at 21:00, but some have different hours.

Your chosen polling station must be situated within your own water board area. And, unless you specially arrange otherwise (see below), it must be within your own municipality.

The website Waar is Mijn Stemlokaal (“Where is my polling station?”) is an interactive map that shows the locations of every polling station in the Netherlands. But the map is not complete until about a week before the election.

Identification

In addition to the stempas, you must show photographic ID to vote. Specifically, you will need your passport, with the sticker or card from the IND that certifies your legal residence in the Netherlands.

If you happen to have an EU/EEA driving licence (Dutch, Polish, etc) then you can identify yourself with that instead of your passport.

Your identity document must not be more than five years out of date. That is, it must be valid until at least 16 March 2018.

Proxy voting

If you can’t vote in person (for example because you will be in Ukraine on 15 March), you can get someone you trust to vote on your behalf. That person must also be eligible to vote, have appropriate identification, and live in the same municipality.

The simplest way to authorize a proxy to vote for you is to complete the form on the back of the stempas. You can then give the stempas to your proxy and they can vote for you. They must show a copy of your passport and IND document – a photo on their phone is OK if everything is clearly readable.

Any one person is limited to cast two proxy votes per election.

The government points out that it is illegal to ask someone to let you vote in their place. This is considered voter fraud and is punishable by imprisonment. If someone asks to buy your vote, report it to the police.

Voting in another municipality

It’s almost always more convenient to vote in your own municipality. But if you really need to vote somewhere else, you can vote in another municipality. You can still only vote at a polling station located within your water board’s area.

You can do this by filling in a form and mailing it to your own municipality, or more simply by visiting the city hall before noon on 14 March. They will exchange your stempas for a “kiezerspas”, which serves the same purpose but can be used in another municipality. This is free of charge.

Choosing how to vote

You probably know very little about Dutch political parties, especially those that provide candidates in waterschap elections. You’re not alone in that. Fortunately, the website Mijn Stem (“My Vote”) is available to provide advice on what party best matches your views.

Mijn Stem is a collaboration between DPG Media Group (which, among other things, owns the newspaper De Volkskrant) and Citisens, a company that does market research but specialises in helping public agencies to improve political engagement. All parties participate in it.

Please note that, while Mijn Stem is usually considered independent and impartial, this cannot be absolutely guaranteed. Once it has made its recommendation, you should review the party’s manifesto to check that you actually agree with it.

The rest of this section is a walkthrough of the questionnaire.

Using Mijn Stem

When you open MS, you will be asked which election you need advice for. Click “Waterschappen”.

You will then be asked for your postcode so that the website can find which waterschap you are voting for. Enter it and click “Check”. After a moment, a new button will appear with the name of your waterschap, so click that.

Once the title screen for the questionnaire has loaded, you can choose to switch language to English using the button at the top-right corner. If you don’t read English, you’ll need to enable your web browser’s translation function.

Several questions have highlighted sections that you can click to get an explanation of terminology.

For each question, indicate your opinion by dragging the slider left or right. You have to move it at least slightly to continue, but you don’t have to move it all the way in either direction. Note that the position of the answers does not signify whether they are politically left- or right-wing answers.

Once you have given an opinion, you can click the button to read manufesto extracts on the topic at hand.

Once you have answered all the questions, you will be asked for your postcode again. You don’t have to answer this. If you do, your opinions will be anonymously added to the researchers’ database. You are also asked for your email address and whether you would like to be asked to participate in future surveys – “Nee” is the Dutch word for “No.” Finally, tick the box that says you agree with the privacy statement and click “Volgende” once more.

The results screen is a list of all the parties running in the election in your area, with the best matches at the top. It shows with a percentage how much it thinks your opinion matches that of the party. You can click any of the parties to see how your answer compares to the party’s for each question.

How to cast your vote

Your polling station should be easy to find. They are usually community halls, places of worship, or libraries – in any case, a large public building. Temporary signage is placed from a considerable distance.

You can bring your child or other non-voters to the polling station, although they aren’t allowed in the voting booth.

When you arrive, you should see a table with several officials (there may be a small queue). Give one of them your stempas (or kiezerspas) and ID.

The official will check that everything matches up. This may take a couple of minutes and may involve discussion with a colleague, so don’t be alarmed if it takes longer than you expect. They will then give you back your ID and a ballot. They keep the stempas. You may also be given a red pencil at this point, or you will find one inside the voting booth. Go into the voting booth alone.

Unfold the ballot, locate your preferred candidate, and use the pencil to fill in the circle next to their name. Do not mark the ballot in any other way.

The Electoral Council would prefer that you refrain from taking a selfie with your ballot. However, it is legal to do so.

Fold up the ballot again (preferably the same way it was folded originally, but this is not very important) and leave the voting booth.

If you have made some kind of mistake, or have changed your mind, then at this point you can return the ballot to a worker for destruction and receive a fresh ballot. You can only do this once.

When you are happy with your ballot, you will need to find the right box for it. You should see two or more boxes labelled something like “WATERSCHAP” and “PROVINCIE” – the former is for your ballot, the latter is for ballots in the provincial election. Place the ballot in the box and return your pencil if applicable.

You can then leave the polling station immediately. Congratulations – you have just voted in the Netherlands for the first time.

Future elections

Elections are quite frequent in the Netherlands, but not everyone can vote in all of them. Here are the upcoming elections:

  • European Parliament: 2024. EU nationality required to vote.

  • House of Representatives (Tweede Kamer): 2025 or earlier. Dutch nationality required.

  • Municipalities (gemeenten): 2026. No nationality requirement.
